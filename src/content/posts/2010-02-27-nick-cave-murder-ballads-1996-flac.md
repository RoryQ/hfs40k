Despite Nick Cave's ongoing obsession with love and death, even much before the release of *Murder Ballads*, this album remains different, specialising in a dense, cathartic, ironic and bloody atmosphere under which Nick Cave painted some of his more unforgiving musical and lyrical portraits.

Bad reviews of the album can actually enhance the listening experience. I can't remember where I read it, but someone's review bashing the album for being immoral made me start to appreciate songs like *Song of Joy* that I had previously overlooked. Not so much there to merely introduce the album, the song is a story about a man whose wife and three children are murdered one night, leaving him an emotionally bankrupt drifter. Fittingly, the protagonists get less sympathetic after that, as well as Cave's role as storyteller. While *Song of Joy* is a heart-wrenching display of love and death, Cave delights in the exploits of anti-hero *Stagger Lee* in the following song, detailing the bad motherfucker's murder-spree "just count the holes in the motherfucker's head!" and perverse homo-eroticism "I'd crawl over 50 good pussies just to get to one fat boy's asshole" and even becomes the killer in the 15 minute seemingly improvised epic *O'Malley's Bar*. In the more musically touching numbers, emotion is still scarce. Guest PJ Harvey's character kills herself on *Henry Lee* because Cave's character won't sleep with her. Pop singer Kylie Monogue's appearance on the song *Where the Wild Roses Grow* is definitely a strange one, as well as the fact that the song actually managed to become a single anywhere! Less detached than in Henry Lee, Cave's character does love, or at least admire Minogue's Elisa Day, but as Cave himself concludes in a video-commentary "I take this woman down to a river and kill her with a rock". The final sympathetic portrait is in my opinion the best song on the album, *the Kindness of Strangers*, in which a young Mary Bellows travels alone from her poor family home in Arkansas to see the ocean. She meets the charming Richard Slade who she asks to leave after he's escorting her to a motel. As she gets lonely and homesick, she undoes the latch on the motel door. Weeping starts in the background as Cave tells us that she was found the next day "cuffed to the bed, rag in her mouth and a bullet in her head". It's what we've all been waiting for as Cave foreshadowed the event in the sporadic chorus "Oh, poor Mary Bellows".

The mood is lifted for the ending, the Bob Dylan cover *Death Is Not The End*, in which the deceased Minogue and Harvey each come back from the dead to sing a verse, as well as muse Anita Lane, good friend Shane McGowan and a few of the Bad Seeds.
