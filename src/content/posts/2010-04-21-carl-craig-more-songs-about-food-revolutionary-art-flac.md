Carl Craig's name demands reverence rare even in the hallowed halls of Detroit Techno, as he is one of the few true links between the sainted Belleville Three (Juan Atkins, Derrick May, and Kevin Saunderson) and subsequent phalanxes of techno artisans. Craig is also an avowed disciple of broody, bed-sit indie fare, making him Detroit's conduit for the synth-stoked melody and melancholy of pop aesthetes like the Smiths, Japan, and Yellow Magic Orchestra. In fusing the wracked, tear-smeared music of the international sigh-and-sob set with the escapist interplanetary electro-funk of his forebears, Craig has become the disconsolate dreamer among Detroit's dancing demigods. While Craig often consigns his visionary productions to DJ-friendlier wax, *More Songs* allows the vinyl-impaired to enter the mindset of this master of mood and melody. Now the masses can know the pained funk-fidgets of such harpsichord and teardrop-synth sketches as "Goodbye World" and the gorgeously despairing "Red Lights." Craig's second album culls from his classics (neither "At Les" nor the Manuel Göttsching-derived "Dominas" has lost an ounce of its subtly saturnine resplendence) and unveils fresh bids for immortality in the romantic jazz-funk filigree of "Televised Green Smoke" and "Dreamland" and in the fluttering ecstasies of "Butterfly" and the May/Craig-penned "Frustration."
