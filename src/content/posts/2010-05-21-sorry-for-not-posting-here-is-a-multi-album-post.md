I wouldn't listen to Philip Glass very often if it weren't for his *Solo Piano*. Everything people criticise minimalism for is accentuated here by his use of a solo piano; the repetition, the soullessness.... but the great irony for me is that in stripping down the already soulless landscape of Glass' signature sound, he creates some of the most melancholic and yet exciting 'modern classical' music I'm yet to hear, without committing any of the sentimental modern classical clichés of I dunno Max Richter, Library Tapes or another name that will offend people so I won't mention.

Metallic Falcons

*Desert Donuts*

Arguably (as in, I agree) the only good thing to come of CocoRosie. As a young man, I liked *Noah's Ark*, thinking that the cryptic lyrics and arrangements were worth interpreting, until on one of their later albums their lyrics became more direct, and they were fucking terrible, so I thought 'maybe they use cryptic lyrics early on because *their ideas fucking blow*' and I was totally right. Then I met this woman who thought she was some sorta spiritual gypsy because she liked Beirut and CocoRosie. She showed me this video of them talking about some dead animals or something. It was fucking corny. It's kinda a worse realisation than when I realised Devendra Banhart was a hipster (remember my review of *Oh Me Oh My*), because then I realised he was a fun-loving guy. The CocoRosie sisters are serious. Alright! Those are bong noises in the back of that song! *She drew a beautiful picture of a whale* is not a good lyric, and *K-hole* is a terrible name for a song. Contrived rhyming is bad, contrived complexity is even worse. That is CocoRosie. A band for 15-16 year old indie boys trying to impress a girl, and boring stoner girls who think they're gypsies. In fact, their only saving grace is the vocals. No, not the one that tries to sound like Bjork. The operatic one.

This is where Metallic Falcons come in. Gone is the Bjork sister! What remains is the operatic one! Describing their sound as *soft metal* or *baby metal*, or something, they sound like a more unsure Sparklehorse at his most frustrating, but with better vocals. Some people will dig this frustrating sound. Ambient, ambient, catchy, with the kind of guitar riffs you played around with when you first turned up the distortion and slid power-chords instead of trying to move them quickly to your favourite Black Flag song. Nah, that's just one song. If I remember correctly, this is quality stuff. Annoyingly, it's hard to get the self-prescribed *soft metal* tag out of your head when looking at their logo. Is that some lame attempt at mimicking a black metal logo? Is this all some big hipster joke? As I mentioned before, CocoRosie were too caught up in self-irony and detachment to release an album that anyone could actually like or understand completely, and when they did, they exposed a void. Is the frustration here to ensure the listener's ear circumnavigates the gigantic hole of *no creative ideas*, or are they just on to something genuinely good? I'll leave it for you to decide.

When I first heard this, it kinda reminded me of Smog.

Real minimal and direct, croaky voice and stuff. Really is a lovely album. I like the cover art too. All his albums have cool album art. Real high contrast black and white. Probably inspired by Ralph Gibson.

I don't like any of his other albums, this one is the most *indie rock*. The other ones have a sort of 'alt-country' (a term I loathe as much as 'post-rock' or 'idm' (not that I don't hate 'indie-rock')) pretense, and I don't dig that. Everything is straight forward here but maybe open to interpretation. Simple things usually are. Isn't that strange? The complexity of simplicity! Or maybe he's not that bright. I dunno. But his voice rulz. Kinda like Smog but more epic and not as good. Everything stays pretty and direct until *Patience* towards the end, which is epic and drunken and voice-breaking. People that like it more can probably describe it to you better. You'll know if it's your kind of thing or not.

Jeanne Lee & Ran Blake

*You Stepped Out of a Cloud*

> In 1961, both pianist Ran Blake and singer [Jeanne Lee](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010095206/http://allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:wjfixqy5ldke) (helped out on two cuts by bassist[George Duvivier](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010095206/http://allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:0zfixqt5ldfe)) made their recording debut with a set of coolly emotional duets. Nearly 28 years later, they had a reunion for this Owl CD, showing the musical growth they had experienced while still sounding quite recognizable; both had found their own musical paths early on. Half of the numbers on the set are originals (including [Lee's](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010095206/http://allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:wjfixqy5ldke) "I Like Your Style"), but it is generally the fresh renditions of standards that are most memorable, including a haunting "You Stepped Out of a Dream," "Where Are You," "You Go to My Head" and "Alone Together." Blake and [Lee](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010095206/http://allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:wjfixqy5ldke) should work together more often.

When I saw John Cale play live I hoped he'd play a set like on *Fragments of a Rainy Season*. Rather, in an attempt to remind us, the audience, that he was still relevant, he played mostly new songs. I didn't complain, he seemed rather enthusiastic playing songs still fresh for him, and I think we all felt that. Regardless of how he tried to convince us that what he was doing was *as* exciting as his work with the Velvet Underground and of course his solo output in the early-mid 70s, I don't know if anyone believed it. There were inevitably more *Velvet Underground & Nico* t-shirts (worn by middle-agers that couldn't've been over 10 when the album was released regardless of their condescending *I remember the day* demeanour (fuck it, we're probably worse boasting about the fact that that audience is not aware of the epic *Sister Ray*)), than um, I don't know, maybe *Paris 1919* t-shirts? Nothing is as iconic as *VU&Nico*, and I still can't tell whether I wanted 40 year old renditions of those songs or not. We like to associate John Cale with noise and drone improvisation, shredding our ears to pieces in *the Black Angel's Death Song*, *Heroin*, and so on. His solo releases were more humble (albeit sometimes *more* experimental), and *Fragments of a Rainy Season* perfectly captures that kind of a feeling. Humble, post-*iwanttosplityourfuckingearsintoshreadswhileipreparemycock* era introspection. And so it begins with a touch of melancholy. I'm sure he feels confident enough, the songs are all acoustic (piano or guitar) and delicate and lovely, but it all feels post-iconic, post-introspective, post-important, post post post post post post post post

Interestingly enough, when Jeff Buckley covered *Hallelujah* he thought he was covering Cale. His version of the song is the last track here.

For fans of his solo material, it's completely essential.
