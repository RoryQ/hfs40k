Herbert Kegel / Werner Haseleu / Reiner Goldberg / Rundfunk-Sinfonie-Orchester Leipzig / Rundfunkchor Leipzig

  
Atonal music. Worse yet, twelve-tone music. Even though nearly a century has passed since the inception of these unfortunate terms (and the awesome music they fail to approximate) they still are pet peeves for countless classical fans. I will admit I have never understood them – not those who merely dislike this music – the ones who feel compelled to boast that the music of Schoenberg, his pupils and their followers was nothing but a failed revolt against all that it beautiful and good in music, suffocated by an arbitrary, self-imposed corset of alien mathematical order that smothered and and all trace of emotion in their works. Their passionate, even desperate attacks against music that has scarcely invaded the concert halls, much less replaced the great composers of the common practice period, have always mystified and intrigued me, they were what made me seek out this music when I was rediscovering classical music for myself a few years ago.  
  
My first exposure to music composed using Schoenberg's twelve-tone technique was Alban Berg's „Lyric Suite“ for string quartet. Upon hearing it I wanted to flinch away from its harshness as much as I wanted to delve deeper into the heart of this alien sound world, so far removed from the tension-relief duality of tonal music, a new, a world where every sound was an entity unto itself, pure expression rather than part of a tonal hierarchy. The more I listened to it (and the less I tired to compare its sounds to those imprinted into my mind by tonal music) the more the lines between consonance and dissonance seemed to blur, no longer a dichotomy but rather purely physical properties of sound. And as initial shock at the absence of hierarchies I had taken for granted faded I began to feel the emotional content of the music – dark humor, blistering eroticism, searing pain! A sinister buffo opera finale for string quartet. But I'm rambling, this post is not about Berg's crowning achievement (which I will post in due time) – it's about what I believe to be Schoenberg's opus summum: The epic opera fragment „Moses and Aron“.  
  
Though Arnold Schoenberg was born to Jewish parents it is doubtful he received any religious training, in his youth he referred to himself as an unbeliever with a deep reverence for the bible, Jesus (in his own words „the purest, most innocent, selfless and idealistic being that ever walked the earth“) and later Moses. There is no evidence that his conversion to Protestantism in 1898 was motivated by career opportunities (unlike Mahler's Catholic baptism for example), the growing European anti-Semitism of the 1920s and the identity crisis that resulted from it renewed Schoenberg's interest in the faith of his ancestors (eventually culminating in his reconversion to Judaism in 1933) and sparked the interest in Moses that would give birth to „Moses and Aron“.  
  
In 1925 he first explores central theme of the opera – the second commandment – in his own words in the Four Pieces for Mixed Choir, Op. 27 No. 2:  
  
„Du sollst dir kein Bild machen! / Denn ein Bild schränkt ein, / begrenzt, faßt / Was unbegrenzt und unvorstellbar bleiben soll.“  
  
(Thou shalt not make for yourself an idol / for an idol constrains / confines, limits / That which should remain unlimited and inconceivable)  
  
Schoenberg first intended to write a short cantata, eventually discarded that idea and began to plan an oratorio - which eventually ended up as an opera. Its libretto is a lengthy meditation on the abstract idea of God and the immediate needs of his people, it explores the dualities between God and Man, thoughts and images, the transcendental and the manifest – these contrasts are embodied by the eponymous protagonists, Moses and Aron. Their conflict is also expressed in the vocal styles assigned to each, Moses coveys his thoughts through rhythmically angular „Sprechstimme“ (spoken words with approximate pitch), Aron through highly expressive, sensuous, aria-like singing. Often the immediate drama takes a backseat to the epic conflict between the aforementioned dualities, only towards the end of the second act the drama becomes more and more tangible – Aron gives in to the demands of the people for a visible God and creates the golden calf, a deed with grave consequences, for the idea of God has been lost on the people and all that remains is projection on an idol from the human mind and its needs. By being lowered to this human level religion is reduced to frenzy, orgy, violence. Moses confronts Aron, who says he acted out of love for his people and further claims the commandments Moses received on the mountain are nothing but further images, fractions of the idea that is God, upon which Moses shatters the stone tablets. As the people are distracted by Aron's miracles Moses realizes his idea of God does no justice to the real God, who is absolutely inconceivable to mankind – the final words Schoenberg set to music are: „O Wort, du Wort, das mir fehlt!“ (O word, o word that I lack!) He did not compose music to the third act of the libretto.  
  
In many ways „Moses and Aron“ sums up Schoenberg's mature period like no other work, it combines rich polyphony with lush, highly expressive harmony and a tasteful, never intrusive sense of motivic coherence, all supported by a single twelve-tone row, its permutations and fragmentations – yes, Schoenberg frequently breaks the strict rules of his twelve-tone technique in this work, just like he pushed the envelope of tonality at the beginning of his career he is now pushing the limits of his own system. This is undoubtedly an expressive choice on Schoenberg's part, for the music that could be deducted from the work's tone-row is impossible to exhaust in the mere 100 minutes of the two completed acts. The pure, unadulterated idea of God is represented by the complete prime form of the tone-row, the more the people stray from it the more fragmented and imperceptible the thematic substance, the interval relationships of the tone-row become. The orchestration is virtuosic, the choral writing is some of the most challenging yet uniquely rewarding in the entire repertoire – the sonorities Schoenberg elicits from the wordless choir in the first scene borders on effects only achievable through electronics.  
  
Herbert Kegel is nearly forgotten nowadays, he was by far the most important champion of 20th century classical in the communist German Democratic Republic, single-handedly shaping the Leipzig Radio Choir into the best German-language choir of the day – one of the few choirs capable of doing justice to this most challenging of all choral operas, it is the combination of Kegel's masterly handling of the score, the virtuosity of his choir and the sense of spontaneity that make me prefer this recording to Solti's smoother, slightly over-rehearsed interpretation. Admittedly Solti's orchestra sounds lusher and more beautiful, however, I feel this isn't always at the service of the music, nor is the singing of his soloists as idiomatic as that of Kegel's, who – above all the Heldentenor Reiner Goldberg – are fantastic and fully aware of what they're singing.  
  
Quite frankly I consider this to be the essential recording of the greatest opera written in the 20th century, a must-hear for all fans of classical music.
