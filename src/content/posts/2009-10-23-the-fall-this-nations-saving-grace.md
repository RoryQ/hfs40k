"Feel the wrath of my Bombast!" exhorts [Smith](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010032850/http://www.allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:gpfexqtgld0e) on this follow-up to their groundbreaking [*Wonderful and Frightening World of... the Fall*](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010032850/http://www.allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=10:hifqxqy5ldhe), and this collection is ample proof of the pure confidence the group had at this time. Stompers like "Barmy," "What You Need," and the mighty "Gut of the Quantifier" are all led by [Brix Smith](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010032850/http://www.allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:jxfpxql5ldse)'s twanging lead hooks, filled by distorted guitars and bludgeoning drums, on top of which [Smith](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010032850/http://www.allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:gpfexqtgld0e) rants with conviction. But it's the departures from this sound that mark the real interest here: The synth-driven "L.A." looks ahead to the Fall's experiments with electronica; "Paint Work" is an impressionist piece interrupted by [Smith](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010032850/http://www.allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:gpfexqtgld0e) accidentally erasing over some of the track at home; and "I Am Damo Suzuki," a tribute to [Can](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010032850/http://www.allmusic.com/cg/amg.dll?p=amg&sql=11:kifwxqw5ldfe)'s lead singer, which borrows its arrangement from several of that group's songs. The Fall sound mysterious, down-to-earth, and hilarious all at the same time. Absolutely essential.
