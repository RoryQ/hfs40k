René Leibowitz / Royal Philharmonic Orchestra  
The Beecham Choral Society  
Inge Borkh / Ruth Siewert / Richard Lewis / Ludwig Weber  
  

This 1961 set of Beethoven symphonies has long been an insider's tip, René Leibowitz – a Polish-French composer, allegedly a student of Webern's and certainly Boulez' most influential teacher – was chosen by legendary Decca producer Charles Gerhardt to conduct the Royal Philharmonic Orchestra in a complete recording of Beethoven's symphonies and major overtures for Reader's Digest, a surprising choice for a series otherwise populated by world-famous conductors like Sir John Barbirolli or Fritz Reiner. Something about Leibowitz' conducting must've left a deep impression on Gerhardt, listening to this cycle it's easy to see why – compared to the Beethoven recordings of his day Leibowitz' take on these works sounds delightfully brash and brisk, he takes Beethoven's metronome marks seriously yet does not conduct with an overly rigid beat, rather than imposing himself on the flow of the music he subtly channels it towards ever loftier heights of expression. The joy of the musicians is palpable, every solo is alive yet nothing feels arbitrary or unformed, indeed, the structural clarity and finality that is so important in Beethoven is omnipresent, Leibowitz savors every climax without losing track of the big picture, no finale sounds underwhelming – not even the tricky ones of Nos. 3 and 7, he does a marvelous job at bringing out the individual character of each symphony, from the gently trickling rhythms of the pastoral No. 6 to the super-charged Rossini of Nos. 2 and 4 to the grimly humorous neoclassical No. 8. While some might find the cycle slightly under-rehearsed I would not have it any other way, for this cycle marries the flawless sound of a studio production with the drama and spontaneity of a live concert. Speaking of the sound – this cycle was recorded by Kenneth Wilkinson (also of Decca fame) and still sounds amazing by today's standards, in terms of depth and naturalness this recording outshines many later ones – the sheer power of the brass/timpani thunder in the first movement of No. 9 is beyond words. Sadly no remastering does full justice to the sound of the original LPs and tapes (yet). This 2004 Scribendum remaster is a step in the right direction, the sound of symphonies Nos. 1, 2, 4 and 6 through 9 is amazingly close to the sound of the 60s commercial tapes I've heard - nowhere as soft and over-fitlered as the 90's Chesky remaster, regrettably the sound of Nos. 3 and 5 isn't quite on par with that of the rest, noticeably flatter and in the case of No. 3 marred by some irritating, irregular tape noise – which is a crying shame, for No. 3 is one of the best performances of the set. It's still perfectly listenable, but given the potential of the original recordings those two remasters are more than disappointing.  
  
But enough words for now – in the end everyone's got their favorite Beethoven cycle, this is mine and who knows – maybe it'll be yours too in a little while. :)
