Using the chronological order each artist appears on the *[Whom The Moon a Nightsong Sings](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010145845/http://holyfuckingshit40000.blogspot.com/2011/04/thank-you-tolkkii.html)*(HFS:40000 Post), I'll progress my intended trend of posting my favorite neofolk work by every artist on that compilation with this lossless rip of Empyrium's 1999 album [*Where at Night the Wood Grouse Plays*](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010145845/http://rateyourmusic.com/release/album/empyrium/where_at_night_the_wood_grouse_plays/), . It's folky acoustic album of Franconian Romanticism that possesses a Pantheistic view of nature.

Surprising is the stylistic change from Empyrium's 1997 album [*Songs of Moors and Misty Fields*](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010145845/http://rateyourmusic.com/release/album/empyrium/songs_of_moors_and_misty_fields/). This is a band that occasionally used the blast-beats and distortion common in black metal as well as screams and prevalent doom and symphonic metal elements, now left with mostly folk instrumentation and the deep, borderline operatic vocals of Ulf Theodor Schwador(Markus Stock). Being stripped of all metal overtones, what is left is a major focus on nylon-stringed guitars with sparse harmonious chanting, conventional singing, the occasional flute and not much else. 3 of the 9 tracks are instrumental and the vocals are rarely necessary to any of the composition, but are still gentle, welcoming and an enjoyable addition.

In terms of feelings, themes, and meaning, not much has changed since Empyrium's last album. They were never aggressive, but there is a certain calmness to this album because of the shorter track lengths and lack of metal tendencies that made their earlier two albums more climatic. The lyrics still deal with the sad embrace of nighttime, the beauty of nature, the pain that comes with nostalgic longing for lost love, and the mystery of death's beyond. There's even a refined/improved version of *When Shadows Grow Longer*, the first track on *Songs of Moors and Misty Fields* and the only purely neofolk song on that album. This re-imagining of that song makes the connection between those two works even stronger and more apparent than it already was.

Although the album isn't lengthy being 32 minutes long, it doesn't need to be. There's so much beauty to be found in the story told by the album. Yes, there is a coherence to the lyrics that forms a unified story and atmosphere. It might not be entirely obvious at first, so I’ll give a brief explanation. The lyrics of the first and self-titled track establish the mood and setting. Track 2(*Dying Brokenhearted*) desrcibes where a woman lays down and dies on a mossy stone under an oak tree. Continuing the story, *The Shepherd And The Maiden Ghost* is a short folk tale about a man who meets the singing ghost of that dead woman. *The Sad Song Of The Wind* is the mournful song of the maiden the man hears. *Wehmut* and *A Pastoral Theme* and *Abendrot* are the melancholy and dreariness during the progression of time and feelings after the shepherd's meeting with the ghost. Those three instrumental tracks build up to the 8th track, *Many Moons Ago*. This song is about the Shepard’s recollection of the night he witnessed the maiden's song and his realization that his precious memory is of her cursed ghost. *When Shadows Grow Longer '99* is when the sun sets, concluding the story.
