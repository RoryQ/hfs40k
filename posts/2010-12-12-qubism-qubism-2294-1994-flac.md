Here's something I've been listening to a lot recently. The style of this album is very *lucid* and it just seems to fit every situation. I'm hesitating to call it 'minimalism' because that implies you'd need some pretentious explanation to get it. It's more like "what you see is what you get", if you catch my drift.  
  
Right at the start you have Teotihuaçan, a 20-minute journey making a link between techno and tribal music, then fading into a nighttime ambient passage at the latter half. This track sets the bar for the rest of the album: it's all about recurring themes in different contexts.  
   Anthropomorphic has a driving melodic riff on top of which other instruments start building. It pretends to fade out at some point but suddenly pops up again at original volume (watch out), that time with more emphasis on rhythm.  
   Angels in T:me is sad as heck. Starts with a woman laughing silently, although in a more inviting than creepy way. The rest of the song is again based on a very memorable melody with cutoff twiddling, backed by bass, pads and SFX. The title comes from the phrase repeated throughout the track, but it's also a reference to the record label [t:me](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010071439/http://www.discogs.com/label/t:me), which predates em:t.  
   It's followed by the most upbeat track, Repercussions. Like waking up after a good sleep and being all focused for the whole day. Calling it ambient would be a bit of a stretch, but that's not to say it feels out of place at all.  
   Ghost of Catalonia, on the other hand, is the real "ambient" piece here. It's mostly sampled sounds of nature with non-intrusive synth backing, although those tribal beats do kick in eventually. Can really make you feel like you're somewhere else if you pay attention.  
   The album ends with Under the Gun, the short oddball piece which is kind of noisy and heavy on effects. I like to believe this album "tells" a story about a tribe chilling out in a jungle and this is the point where civilized people come wreck their homes.
