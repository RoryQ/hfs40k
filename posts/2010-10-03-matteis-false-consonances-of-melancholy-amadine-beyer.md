(Amadine Beyer / Gli Incogniti)

  
So far I've only posted music by big-name composers, time to turn the spotlight onto some lesser-known names - the first being Nicola Matteis, an Italian-born composer who apparently spent his youth as a wandering virtuoso, supposedly he traversed all of Europe on foot before settling in England eventually. His music is as cosmopolitan as it is unique and personal, it aptly marries the racy coarseness of Neapolitan folk music and the refined, mannered style that was popular in France and England at the time - his music is informed by his own virtuosity at all times that never feels tacked on or forced, it always contributes to the subtle playfulness of his music - like Haydn after him Matteis seems to have had a lot of fun toying with the musical stereotypes of his era, freely twisting and fusing different dance forms and adorning them with verbose, occasionally humorous titles. His harmonic writing is also highly original and unusually chromatic for his day, he particularly favors the augmented triad, an interval that sounded quite different from the perfect fourth in the tuning system of his day, faithfully replicated by Beyer and her ensemble - she studied Matteis' writings on performance practice extensively before approaching this music, the results are stunningly idiomatic and vivid, there is no trace of academic dryness in her spirited playing - every note is alive, even the continuo is a joy to listen to. But why am I still wasting your time with my writing? You need this life-enhancer right now.
