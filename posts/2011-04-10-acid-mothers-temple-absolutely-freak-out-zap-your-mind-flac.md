Released just before the deluge -- in 2000, you still had to hunt out Acid Mothers Temple albums in the specialist importers, now they're everywhere -- Absolutely Freak Out was the band's best album since the astonishing surprise of their debut in 1997 and is still the second studio album you should buy from these lysergic monsters. Start, however, with their 2001 Live In Japan album, complete with Mothers of Invention "pencil cover" parody and burning, aching, soul-shredding 40 minute live version of "La Novia" which stomps all over the somewhat tedious studio version and leaves you cowering on the floor somewhere among the broken splinters of Kawabata's electric guitar. To say this is the best live band in the world today is an understatement -- this is the best live band in the world in the last twenty five years.  
  
The strength of Absolutely Freak Out lies in its diversity. The first, untitled AMT album impressed because it was one continuous track which lost you somewhere within the 20-minute heavy sludge at the beginning, then took what was left on a wild roller-coaster trip before lasering away what little shreds of brain remained with the sustained neural damage of the title track's sine wave, a kind of homage to Zappa's "Weasels Ripped My Flesh". In Absolutely Freak Out (Zap Your Mind!!) the homage is still there in title at least, but this is a band which casts its psychedelic net far and wide. Arguably, the farthest points were explored back in the early 1970s, and it's hard to imagine how psychedelic mayhem could go any further out than, say, Ash Ra Tempel or AMM. Arguably, also, all psychedelia since then has had to work within those limits, just as jazz has stepped back from the free experiments of the early 1960s -- where else can you go? So just as the best psychedelic pop bands of the 1990s have reinvented the great psychedelic pop music of the 1960s -- Olivia Tremor Control in particular -- the best psychedelic noise bands of the 1990s have done the same with Krautrock, Pink Floyd, and the likes of Terry Brooks and Cromagnon, while laying on every guitar effect ever devised at once so that what is produced is a grungy, ill-focused roar which owes more to Terry Riley than Hawkwind.  
  
More recent albums -- "Electric Heavy Land" in particular -- have seemed so slavishly overdriven they've threatened to be swallowed by their own dark star, but Absolutely Freak Out offers more than plain noise. There are some beautiful gentle sequences, touches of peace among the maelstrom, which make this a satisfying double set. Still, don't expect many tracks to be less than 20 minutes long. Best of the lot, actually, is one of the CD-only additions to the original double album: "Stone Stoner" is a mixture of Cotton Casino's little-girl-lost vocals and Kawabata's sudden, brutal, impossibly destructive electric guitar (a bit like a slowed-down version of Jimmy Page's solo in "In The Evening") erupting in your face like you're strapped down in a fireworks factory during an air raid; everything goes off at once, and while you're still dazed and seeing stars, the piece evaporates in a series of spiralling synth reverbs that take Conrad Schnitzler space outs to a ludicrous extreme, and then some. Which is, probably, the point: the homage/parody covers, silly titles and the band's Ya Ho Weh image (not to mention the recurring naked girls on the covers -- not so much non-PC as blissfully nostalgic) all point toward a keen knowledge of psychedelia's farthest limits, and a willingness to skirt those distances with a smile on their shaggy faces. Don't take it seriously, just submit with a laugh. How Kawabata still has anything resembling a sense of hearing is beyond me. Buy one for your granny and she won't knit you any more jumpers. Perfection.
