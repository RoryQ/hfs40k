This is Partch's life-time masterpiece.   
  
It sums up all his obsessions in a perfectly realized 72-minute composition/performance/ritual.   
  
Partch was a true musical maverick. He constructed his own 43-note-to-the-octave scale. Over the course of decades, the former hobo and carpenter built dozens of instruments in order to play music for his scale. The instruments, often carved of wood or assembled of found-parts, are works of art in themselves: Cloud Chamber Bowls, Spoils of War, Eroica Marimba, an array of gourds and zithers and Hoo-ville noisemakers come to life. The sounds they conjure resemble nothing else in music.   
  
Crucially, the instruments are never showcased as novelties. Instead, they're seamlessly integrated into the music; they exist simply to construct and support the drama unfolding on stage. One half of the spectacle is based on a Japanese folk tale; it's about reconciling death with on-going life. The other half is based on an African folk tale; it's a farce, and deals with carrying on in everyday life despite society's absurdities. The net result is wonderful art -- enchanting and playful, solemn and humane, and utterly timeless. *Delusion of the Fury* sounds like it could have been performed hundreds of years ago in an alternate civilization. On the other hand, it will still sound futuristic one hundred years hence.
