[![](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010175317im_/http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/51XI%2BSEvOgL._SL500_AA300_.jpg)](https://web.archive.org/web/20111010175317/http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/51XI%2BSEvOgL._SL500_AA300_.jpg)I've been on a Bartók binge lately, so it's only right that I post one of his most recognized masterworks. I also have a reading of this by Szell with his famous Cleveland Orchestra that I might up later on so that you all can do comparisons, but from listening to both I can say that there really isn't one I prefer over the other.

In composing this piece, Bartók incorporated a diverse array of influences to arrive at this paradoxically titled (a concerto is a work for solo instrument accompanied by orchestra) whole. Drawing on folk songs from his Hungarian background, the classical (period) approach to sonata form, and a modern sense of tonality (yet a not-completely-out-there sense), I view this piece as the best exemplar of Bartók's position in my mind as one of the best retrograde-thinking forward-thinkers the world of classical music has yet seen, and certainly one of the top five composers of the 20th century.

An interesting bit of musical trivia: Bartók was not a fan of Shostakovich's Symphony No. 7 (Leningrad) -- a work that was experiencing much popularity during the war (the Concerto for Orchestra was composed in 1943) but whose reputation would wane after it -- and thus in the fourth movement a parody of one of the themes in the latter symphony can be heard. Composer beef!
