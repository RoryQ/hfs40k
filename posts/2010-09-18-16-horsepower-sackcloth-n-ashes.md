"Sackcloth 'n' Ashes" has the fevered, stream-of-consciousness lyricism of The Birthday Party, and the backwoods appeal of "outlaw" country music. You could film a sequel to "Deliverance," and this would be the soundtrack. It's a great album for college students who are burned out on Dave Matthews and Phish, but aren't quite ready for Swans.  
  
There's little humor in the songs, but it's easier to get in the groove when the singer doesn't play it cute. David Eugene Edwards has a wailing urgency perfect for no-tech music. There's an Old Testament anguish in the singing and the guitar, so the image of hardship and minimal education holds up pretty well. Jean-Yves Tola and Edwards have some fun with the drums and banjo. The yearning, moonshine quality of Edwards' voice reminds me of Concrete Blonde's Johnette Napolitano. All the songs are fairly brief, but the mood is set early and just gets deeper. The black-and-white photos and turn-of-the-century typeface cement the vibe.  
  
As the editor's review states, the lyrics are more for effect than for narrative cohesion: "Every man is evil yes / An[d] every man a liar / An[d] unashamed with wicked tongues / sing in the black soul choir." Still, it works; backwoods musicians (or pseudo-backwoods; the band is from Colorado) sing and play without post-modern irony. The songs are a way to get through the heat and isolation and waiting for the sheriff to find Viola's body in the marsh.  
  
"Horse Head" has a jangly guitar sound and swing beat right out of the late fifties. "Harm's Way" sounds like Buckwheat Zydeco doing a They Might Be Giants song. "American Wheeze" fakes us out with a gentle rim-tap beat, then offers a chorus sure to fill the dancefloor. The bouncy beat in several tracks suggests that these guys aren't totally miserable. "Red Neck Reel" is a foot-stompin' hoot. "Prison Shoe Romp" is a chain-gang rocker that would fit on Concrete Blonde's "Bloodletting."  
  
These songs don't cover a broad territory, but they're effective. Even if Sixteen Horsepower is pushing an image, they back it up with good songs. They got the important part right.
